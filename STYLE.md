# The Tekmerion: a style guide

*What a Tekmerion is, and how to write one. This governs the prose and the
evidence. The README governs file mechanics and site chrome.*

*Revised from the published Tekmeria (2026), working backwards from the
strongest examples rather than policing a prior checklist.*

---

## What a Tekmerion is

A **Tekmerion** (τεκμήριον, plural **Tekmeria**) is an evidence-led corpus
note. It begins from a question about what the Eulogikon corpus contains, then
sets down the passages that answer that question: original Greek, transliteration,
translation, and enough framing for the reader to know what they are looking at.

It is not a conventional essay with a thesis imposed on the evidence. It is also
not a bare concordance. The best Tekmeria rearrange something the reader thought
they already knew (an English gloss, an empty search, an expected spokesman,
a familiar lemma) by placing witnesses until that rearrangement is visible.

The writer may orient the reader. The writer may name a visible pattern. The
writer may say where a term changes function, where a later witness preserves an
earlier usage, or where the corpus does not contain an expected phrase. What the
writer must not do is make the evidence carry more than it actually bears, or
put a finding in a mouth that never said it.

Leave the reader with the evidential landscape: what appears, where it appears,
how the witnesses stand beside one another, and what the corpus cannot show.

---

## The balance to keep

The form walks a narrow path.

| Too dry | The aim | Too much |
|---|---|---|
| Lemma entry, period table, no hinge | Informative and interesting | Miniature monograph or manifesto |
| Passages listed without a reason to read | Suggestive | Speculative synthesis the stations were recruited to illustrate |
| Fear of any framing | Honest | Admiration language, crowning verdicts, invented spokesmen |

**Informative** means checkable stations: who, what text, what kind of witness,
what the line does on the page.

**Interesting** means a hinge early: the received question or gloss is not quite
the real center. Marcus looks lexical (which word for prayer?) and the interest
is one step further (he keeps the word and redirects its object). The
Hippocratic piece looks like another ἐνέργεια note and the center is ἀεργοί /
ἐνεργοί in craft and clinic.

**Suggestive** means naming a pattern the arrangement makes visible, without
forcing a doctrine. Aletheia's closing "shared shape" earns its place because
the verbs and contrasts have already been shown station by station, and the
caveats refuse a single philosophical definition. Suggestive is allowed.
Speculative is not: do not invent what no witness holds, and do not write as if
the corpus had announced your reading.

**Honest** means station type, absence, gap, and provenance are first-class.
Doxography is not original speech. Author floruit is not composition date for
pseudepigrapha. An empty noun-search is not an empty topic if adjacent lemmas
carry the load (*philía*). No one needs to say "this is not yet metaphysics"
if idle tools beside a missing noun beside Plato's silence already show it.

## Exemplars (work backwards from these)

**Primary model: What Marcus Aurelius prays for.**  
One controlling question with a hinge; presence and absence both count; every
station earns its place; ending is checkable landscape. Pulse without
hyperbole. "The word is conservative; the content is not."

**Primary model for use-across-time without a spokesman: ἀεργοί and ἐνεργοί.**  
Center of gravity in Hippocratic craft/medicine; Aristotle as cliff, not hero;
apparent early noun hits collapsed by station checks; finding belongs to the
arrangement, not to anyone's mouth.

**High-ambition edge: Ἀλήθεια (the published piece).**  
English gloss refused; wide genre spread so philosophy does not monopolize;
verbs and opposites tracked; a shared shape named at the end, then caveated.
Use this ceiling when the witnesses really share a shape. Do not use the draft
habit of knowing the metaphysics ("kinetic substrate") before the stations
finish speaking.

**Strong supporting models:**

- *paráklētos*: single semantic arc; root holds, domain expands; gap as evidence.
- *Περὶ φιλίας*: empty search ≠ absence; fragment provenance named without apology.
- *τετράγωνος*: morphological precision and corpus absence (*ἡ τετράγωνος ψυχή*
  does not occur); quotation afterlife kept in scale.
- *What Aristotle attributes to Socrates*: single-locus deep dive; tensions left
  unresolved; proves the form need not be a full diachronic survey.

**Cautionary (serviceable, thinner as a model):**

- *kólasis*: clear layers, but closer to a lemma entry ending in a definition;
  local reading of witnesses is lighter; less distinctive hinge than Marcus or
  Aletheia.

When unsure, write more like Marcus or ἀεργοί / ἐνεργοί than like a dictionary
article or a Heideggerian essay.

## One question, one hinge, one trajectory

Every Tekmerion should have one controlling corpus question. Prefer a question
with a hinge: the first answer is true but incomplete, and the interest lies one
step further on.

Good controlling questions (from published pieces):

- Which word does Marcus use for prayer? (Interest: he keeps it and redirects
  the object.)
- What was already doing the work of "at work / effective" before the noun
  ἐνέργεια sits securely? (Interest: Hippocratic adjective and verb; noun
  absent there.)
- Does English *truth* fit what the corpus does with *alḗtheia*? (Interest:
  verbs, opposites, domains.)
- Do the Later Stoics talk about love? (Interest: nouns sparse; adjacent lemmas
  and early Stoa carry the topic.)

Weaker as a sole question: "Survey *kólasis* diachronically." That can still
work, but needs a sharper hinge (gloss vs corrective strand, or parallel
biblical tension) stated early.

The body follows one clear trajectory. Chronology is often best, not mandatory.
A trajectory can be lexical, conceptual, textual, or evidential:

- Focal author deepened, with earlier stations preparing the redirect (Marcus)
- Craft/medical family -> rhetorical noun -> corpus absences -> technical cliff
  (ἀεργοί / ἐνεργοί)
- Genre-spread across centuries so one school does not stand for the whole
  (Aletheia)
- Empty late search, then roll back to founders (*philía*)
- Earliest witness -> later quotation -> parallel report -> corpus absence
  (*tetrágōnos*)

The trajectory is arrangement that makes the hinge readable, not an argument
that forces a conclusion.

Two viable centers of gravity:

1. **Focal author or one locus** (Marcus; Aristotle 1078b).
2. **Word or image tracked until the landscape is visible** (*alḗtheia*,
   *paráklētos*, ἀεργοί / ἐνεργοί).

Both are Tekmeria if they serve a corpus question. The form is not "always a
lemma from archaic to late antique."

## Stay close to the evidence

The old danger is overclaiming: thesis, manifesto, or miniature reference
article. The opposite danger is overcorrection: a list of passages with no felt
reason to read it.

The rule is not "never interpret." The rule is: interpret only at the distance
the evidence allows, and prefer suggestion that the reader can re-check.

Allowed (local, on the page):

> The contrast is already visible. Earth returns downward; *pneûma* rises.

> Four occurrences across three passages. The word is conservative; the content
> is not.

> ἀεργοί and ἐνεργοί are opposites in a craft image. Nothing metaphysical is in
> play.

Allowed (pattern named from arranged stations):

> What holds these together is not a shared translation but a shared shape:
> *alḗtheia* is the standing-open of what might have stayed hidden.

Only when the stations have already shown the verbs and contrasts, and caveats
refuse a single crowning definition.

Avoid:

> This is the decisive transformation of the entire tradition.

> The Stoic innovation absorbs and transcends all previous uses.

> What is striking here is the astonishing survival of the archaic sense.

> The Greeks thought that *phýsis* does not mean nature.

(The last may be the finding of a piece; it still must not be put in a
historical mouth. Leave it as landscape: pairings, contrasts, domains, and
where English *nature* fails.)

Prefer verbs that describe what the passage does on the page: pairs,
distinguishes, equates, preserves, applies, quotes, glosses, defines,
contrasts, repeats, omits, redirects, retains, collapses (of a false hit).
Be sparing with verbs that announce judgment: proves, reveals, transforms,
transcends, revolutionizes.

## Framing that earns its place

Framing is part of the form. Without it, the reader cannot know what kind of
witness they are reading. Framing supplies interest; it must stay honest.

Useful framing includes:

- **The station.** Who is speaking, in what text, and what kind of source this
  is: dialogue, medical treatise, testimonium, scholium, lexicon, commentary,
  fragment preserved by a later author, hostile report.
- **The local fact.** What word is paired with what, what grammatical form is
  used, what object receives the adjective, what contrast is made in the line.
- **The visible shift.** A later passage defines what an earlier passage
  equates; a medical passage keeps a physical usage while philosophical texts
  technicalize the term; a quotation becomes an anthology item.
- **The corpus boundary.** Expected phrase absent; witness late; work only
  through quotation; author floruit misleading for pseudepigrapha; Plato (or
  another corpus) returns zero.
- **The hinge restated lightly.** After a block of stations, one sentence that
  returns to why these witnesses sit together, without crowning a theory.

Bad framing tells the reader what to admire. Good framing tells the reader what
they are looking at, and occasionally what the arrangement makes newly visible.

## Evidence blocks

Every Greek witness gets three lines. A reader without Greek must be able to
follow the witness.

1. **The original Greek**, exactly as the corpus yields it, kept and never
   replaced.
2. **A transliteration**, normally ALA-LC: η -> ē, ω -> ō, θ -> th, φ -> ph,
   χ -> ch, ψ -> ps, ξ -> x, rough breathing -> h.
3. **An English translation**, without quotation marks around the whole line.

The Greek is the evidence; the transliteration lets the non-reader hear it; the
translation lets them read it. All three belong together.

Long witnesses may be excerpted when the omitted material is not needed for the
point at hand. Mark omissions plainly with ellipses in the Greek and in the
translation where needed.

## Greek in English prose

Use transliteration in running English by default: *pneûma*, *psychē*,
*tetrágōnos*, *kólasis*, *energós*. Give a plain-English gloss where useful.

Greek script is allowed in running prose when exact form matters:

- morphology: `τετράγωνον` is neuter, not feminine;
- corpus absence: the phrase `ἡ τετράγωνος ψυχή` does not occur;
- lexical contrast: `πνεῦμα` is paired with `ἀήρ` in the witness;
- table cells where the Greek form itself is the datum.

Do not make English prose unreadable for a reader without Greek. If a sentence
uses Greek script, give enough transliteration, gloss, or context for the
sentence to remain legible.

Translations should usually be English. Use Greek script in a translation only
when the untranslated form is itself the object of the note.

**No em dashes (U+2014).** Repunctuate with colons, commas, parentheses, or
middle dots (·). En dashes (U+2013) in reference ranges (e.g. `1078b17–31`) are
fine. Details and examples are in the README.

## Shape and length

Keep the writer's voice economical. Let the number and density of witnesses set
the total length. Interest comes from the hinge and the framing, not from
padding.

A short Tekmerion may have three witnesses and a small "On the evidence"
ending. A larger one may need a diachronic table, a sources table, and caveats.
The danger is not length by itself. The danger is unfocused genre drift:
etymology, dictionary entry, philosophical survey, medical survey, and
theological history all competing inside one piece.

When a draft feels too broad, narrow it by asking:

- What is the one corpus question, and what is its hinge?
- Which witnesses are essential to that hinge?
- Which later materials are only afterlife or background?
- Can the ending be reduced to checkable points (and at most one earned
  suggestive shape)?

When a draft feels too dry, ask:

- Does the opening rearrange a gloss, search result, or expectation?
- Does every station earn its place, or could three drop without loss?
- Is there one sentence of pulse that stays honest (Marcus: "The word is
  conservative; the content is not")?

When a draft feels hyperbolic, ask:

- Am I naming what the passage does, or announcing a tradition-wide event?
- Would this sentence survive if the reader only checked the Greek block above
  it?
- Have I invented a spokesman for a landscape finding?

## Endings

Prefer restrained evidential endings. Good headings include:

- **On the evidence**
- **What the passages show**
- **Corpus boundaries**
- **Caveats and open questions**

The ending should usually leave checkable facts:

- Marcus keeps εὔχομαι / εὐχή and omits the three alternatives.
- The noun ἐνέργεια does not appear in the Hippocratic corpus; the adjective
  and verb do.
- The phrase ἡ τετράγωνος ψυχή does not occur.
- Chrysippus on *alḗtheia* as a body is doxographical, not a treatise excerpt.

An earned suggestive close (Aletheia's "what *alḗtheia* was like") is allowed
when it restates patterns already shown and is followed by caveats that refuse
a single definition. It is not a substitute for the evidential ending.

Tables are useful when they make the evidential landscape easier to see. Do not
include a table merely because a table is available. Prefer not to end with a
dictionary-style definition unless the piece's hinge is precisely the failure of
a modern gloss (and even then, prefer landscape over "a definition that fits").

## Before composing

- **Recall prior Tekmeria and lexicon entries.** A reading already taken should
  inform the new witness, not be silently reinvented or contradicted.
- **Check terminology.** Avoid importing later scholastic or theological
  concepts into earlier witnesses unless the passage itself warrants them.
- **Resolve every corpus reference against the database.** Confirm the passage
  is really at the reference, in the form claimed. Never compose a citation
  from memory. Mechanics are in the README.
- **Decide the center of gravity.** If the early evidence is the point, do not
  let late-antique afterlife take over the piece. If Aristotle is only the
  cliff, do not let him become the subject. If the late-antique development is
  the point, say so at the start.
- **Sort the word-family when needed.** Adjective, verb, and noun may tell
  different stories (ἐνεργός / ἐνεργέω / ἐνέργεια). A noun-only survey can miss
  the hinge.
- **Test apparent early hits for station type.** Testimonia, floruit traps, and
  mis-tagged quotations are part of the evidence story when they matter; they
  are not witnesses of the attributed author.

## Citations

Each primary witness needs both the linked work title and the visible `wid`:

> Demosthenes, *On the Embassy*, Eulogikon: fsm-cc, ref. Or19 1

The `wid` is the stable key. The reference locates the passage inside the work.
Resolve the URL from the database; never hand-guess a slug.

### One citation per witness

Each Greek blockquote is one witness: one station in the corpus. It gets one
citation, placed above the three lines in `<p class="witness-ref">`.

Optional framing prose may sit above the citation. Commentary follows the
translation.

Do not repeat the reference in the framing prose and again after the
translation. Do not bury the station identifier below the evidence while
duplicating it in the lead-in.

Inline `<span class="cite">` is for secondary pointers only: parallel
witnesses, cross-references, lexicographical glosses, or corpus-boundary notes.
It is not for the primary witness block.

---

*A Tekmerion is evidence arranged until a received understanding shifts: not a
thesis imposed on the corpus, not a list without a pulse, and not a finding put
in a mouth that never spoke it.*
