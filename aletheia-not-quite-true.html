Ἀλήθεια: the word that fights, lights, and is a body

Τεκμήρια · Eulogikon · 13 July 2026 · alḗtheia from Homer to the Stoics

A common English gloss for alḗtheia (ἀλήθεια) is truth, understood as a correct statement matching the way things are. This survey sets that gloss beside the actual use and records instead what the corpus does with the word: the verbs it governs, the things it is set against, the domains it travels through. The word is formed from the privative ἀ- and the root of λανθάνω/lanthanō, to escape notice, to be hidden, so on its face alḗtheia is the state of what is not hidden. The passages below, from Homer to the Stoa, test what that comes to across nine centuries and five kinds of writing, and they are drawn widely, from the poets, the natural philosophers, the tragedians, the historians, the physicians, and the orators, so that the philosophers take their place among many hands rather than standing in for the whole.

Homer: alḗtheia is what one lays out in speech (archaic)

At the root of the tradition the word is the object of verbs of telling. In the Odyssey a speaker declares he will set alḗtheia out in full:

Homer, Odyssey, Eulogikon: abe-ab, ref. 11.507

πᾶσαν ἀληθείην μυθήσομαι, ὥς με κελεύεις·

pâsan alētheíēn mythḗsomai, hṓs me keleúeis;

the whole alḗtheia I shall set out in speech, just as you bid me.

The verb is μυθήσομαι/mythḗsomai, I shall tell, recount, deliver in speech; alḗtheia is what one recounts entire, πᾶσαν/pâsan, the whole of it. The same pairing recurs a dozen times: alḗtheia is what a speaker καταλέξω/kataléxō, lays out point by point. And the corpus fixes its opposite directly. A few books on, faithless men will not deliver it:

Homer, Odyssey, Eulogikon: abe-ab, ref. 14.125

ψεύδοντ’ οὐδ’ ἐθέλουσιν ἀληθέα μυθήσασθαι.

pseúdont’ oud’ ethélousin alēthéa mythḗsasthai.

they play false and will not set out alēthéa in speech.

The contrast is with ψεύδεσθαι/pseúdesthai, to deceive, to play false. To deliver alḗtheia is the opposite of concealing, of holding back, of dressing a thing otherwise; the etymology and the use agree at the origin. Alḗtheia is not yet a property a statement has; it is what a speaker does or fails to do when they bring a matter fully into the open.

Heraclitus: speaking and acting alḗtheia by nature (archaic)

In the earliest philosophical stratum the word attaches not only to speech but to conduct, and both are measured against φύσις/phýsis, nature:

Heraclitus of Ephesus, On the Nature of the Universe, Eulogikon: bky-ab, ref. 112

σωφρονεῖν ἀρετὴ μεγίστη, καὶ σοφίη ἀληθέα λέγειν καὶ ποιεῖν κατὰ φύσιν ἐπαΐοντας.

sōphroneîn aretḕ megístē, kaì sophíē alēthéa légein kaì poieîn katà phýsin epaḯontas.

sound sense is the greatest excellence, and wisdom is to say and to do alēthéa, listening to nature.

Here alēthéa is the object of both λέγειν/légein, to say, and ποιεῖν/poieîn, to do. It is not confined to statements; one enacts it as well as speaks it, and the standard for both is κατὰ φύσιν/katà phýsin, in accord with the way things grow and are. To be in alḗtheia is to be aligned with nature in word and deed at once, which no account of it as sentence-correctness reaches.

Empedocles: the meadow of Alḗtheia against the meadow of Ruin (classical)

Empedocles gives the word a place in a moral cosmology, set as a destination against its opposite:

Empedocles of Acragas, On Nature and Perception, Eulogikon: die-ab, ref. 121

ἡ δὲ ἔφεσις τοῦ φεύγοντος τὸν τῆς Ἄτης λειμῶνα πρὸς τὸν τῆς Ἀληθείας ἐπείγεται λειμῶνα.

hē dè éphesis toû pheúgontos tòn tês Átēs leimôna pròs tòn tês Alētheías epeígetai leimôna.

the striving of the one in flight from the meadow of Átē presses on toward the meadow of Alḗtheia.

Ἄτη/Átē is ruin, the blind delusion that leads to destruction; Alḗtheia is named as its opposite pole, a λειμών/leimṓn, a meadow, a place the soul in flight ἐπείγεται/epeígetai, presses hard toward. The word is a region one moves into by leaving delusion behind. It is spatial and it is a goal of motion, not a match between proposition and fact.

Aeschylus: the boast full of alḗtheia (classical)

Tragedy puts the word where a thing shows itself as it is under pressure. In the Agamemnon a beacon-speech is called a boast filled with it:

Aeschylus, Agamemnon, Eulogikon: emo-ae, ref. 610.4

τοιόσδ’ ὁ κόμπος, τῆς ἀληθείας γέμων,

toiósd’ ho kómpos, tês alētheías gémōn,

such is the vaunt, laden with alḗtheia,

γέμων/gémōn is brimming, loaded like a ship to the waterline; the boast is weighed down with alḗtheia as with cargo. The word names something a speech can be full of, a ballast that makes it hold. A fragment of the same poet, preserved in the corpus, says it plainly:

Aeschylus the Tragedian, Fragments on the Palici and Thebes, Eulogikon: emo-ah, ref. book 176

ἁπλᾶ γάρ ἐστι τῆς ἀληθείας ἔπη

haplâ gár esti tês alētheías épē

for the words of alḗtheia are simple.

The words of alḗtheia are unforced, without the folds that concealment adds.

Sophocles: the strength of alḗtheia (classical)

In Oedipus the King Tiresias, pressed and threatened, answers with the word's own force:

Sophocles, Oedipus the King, Eulogikon: buo-ad, ref. 365.5

εἴπερ τί γ’ ἔστι τῆς ἀληθείας σθένος.

eíper tí g’ ésti tês alētheías sthénos.

if indeed there is any strength in alḗtheia.

σθένος/sthénos is might, the strength of a body that can prevail. Alḗtheia is credited with it, as a power that can hold against a king. The same poet, in the Trackers, shows the reverse case, where it can be overpowered:

Sophocles, Trackers, Eulogikon: buo-ai, ref. book 86.2

τό τοι νομισθὲν τῆς ἀληθείας κρατεῖ.

tó toi nomisthèn tês alētheías krateî.

what is taken for granted prevails over alḗtheia.

κρατεῖ/krateî is masters, gets the upper hand. Only something with force of its own can be described as prevailing or being prevailed over; the word sits among contenders, not among labels.

Euripides: the light and the road of alḗtheia (classical)

Euripides gives the word two figures that make the un-concealment concrete. In Iphigenia among the Taurians it is light against the cover of night:

Euripides, Iphigenia in Tauris, Eulogikon: cbm-ac, ref. 1025.2

κλεπτῶν γὰρ ἡ νύξ, τῆς δ’ ἀληθείας τὸ φῶς.

kleptôn gàr hē nýx, tês d’ alētheías tò phôs.

night belongs to thieves, but the light to alḗtheia.

Night is the element of κλέπται/kléptai, thieves, who work by concealment; τὸ φῶς/tò phôs, the light, belongs to alḗtheia, the condition in which things lie open to sight. A fragment gives the paired image of a path:

Euripides, Fragments on Exile and Lineage, Eulogikon: cbm-am, ref. book 289.2

χρεὼν δόλοισι κλέπτειν· τῆς δ’ ἀληθείας ὁδὸς

chreṑn dóloisi kléptein; tês d’ alētheías hodòs

one must steal by tricks; but the road belongs to alḗtheia.

Light and open road on the one side, concealment and stealth on the other; the word marks the state of the uncovered.

Herodotus: dealing in alḗtheia or in what pleases (classical)

The historian sets the word against a rival at the moment of counsel. Xerxes asks Demaratus for his judgment, and the Greek asks which he is to give:

Herodotus, Histories, Eulogikon: elu-aa, ref. 7.101

Βασιλεῦ, κότερα ἀληθείῃ χρήσωμαι πρὸς σὲ ἢ ἡδονῇ;

Basileû, kótera alētheíēi chrḗsōmai pròs sè ḕ hēdonêi?

King, shall I deal with you in alḗtheia or in what gives pleasure?

The verb χρήσωμαι/chrḗsōmai is to use, to employ, to deal in, as one deals in a currency; alḗtheia is set against ἡδονή/hēdonḗ, what is pleasant to hear. To deal in alḗtheia is to bring the matter out undisguised even when disguise would please. It is a way of conducting oneself toward another, weighed against flattery, not a property a report carries.

The physicians: the alḗtheia of a disease (classical)

The medical writers use the adjective for the real state of a body as against its misleading appearance. In the Epidemics a case is marked off as the genuine article:

Hippocrates and the Hippocratic Corpus, Epidemics, Book IV, Eulogikon: bww-ck, ref. 4.13

Ἕτερος τὴν αὐτὴν ὥρην ἀληθεῖ τυφωμανίῃ·

Héteros tḕn autḕn hṓrēn alētheî typhōmaníēi;

another, in the same season, with an alēthḗs delirium;

ἀληθής/alēthḗs here qualifies a τυφωμανίη/typhōmaníē, a bewildered delirium: the physician marks this case as the real condition and not a resembling one, the disease as it actually stands under the confusing surface. In clinical use the word does the work of separating the state that is truly present from the one that merely shows. It is diagnostic, a judgment about what is really the case in a body, and it keeps the sense of a thing brought out from under what hides it.

The court: alḗtheia as fighting ally (classical)

The orators put the word in the battle line. Antiphon, in a homicide speech, argues that a false informer would have had no avenger for alḗtheia, because the one whose ally it was chanced to be absent:

Antiphon of Athens, On the Murder of Herodes, Eulogikon: bqs-ab, ref. Caed Her 37

οὐκ ἂν ἦν αὐτῷ τῆς ἀληθείας τιμωρός, ὅτῳ σύμμαχος ἡ ἀλήθεια ἔτυχεν οὖσα ἀπών.

ouk àn ên autôi tês alētheías timōrós, hótōi sýmmachos hē alḗtheia étychen oûsa apṓn.

there would have been no avenger of alḗtheia for him, the one whose ally alḗtheia chanced to be, since he was absent.

σύμμαχος/sýmmachos is the fighting ally, the one who stands in the line beside another; τιμωρός/timōrós is the avenger who exacts a reckoning. Alḗtheia takes a side and fights on it, and can be left without a champion if its man is away. The forensic sense keeps the same shape as the tragic: a force that contends, not a state of affairs.

The oration: alḗtheia as the good order of speech (classical)

Gorgias, opening a display piece, sets out the pairs a speech must keep, and names the one proper to its account:

Gorgias of Leontini, Helen's Encomium, Eulogikon: eme-ab, ref. 11 (tit)

κόσμος πόλει μὲν εὐανδρία, σώματι δὲ κάλλος, ψυχῇ δὲ σοφία, πράγματι δὲ ἀρετή, λόγῳ δὲ ἀλήθεια·

kósmos pólei mèn euandría, sṓmati dè kállos, psychêi dè sophía, prágmati dè aretḗ, lógōi dè alḗtheia;

the good order of a city is a wealth of good men, of a body its beauty, of a soul its wisdom, of a deed its excellence, and of speech its alḗtheia.

κόσμος/kósmos is the fitting order, the arrangement that makes a thing what it should be; each item has the kósmos proper to it, and alḗtheia is what stands to λόγος/lógos, speech, as beauty stands to a body and excellence to a deed. It is the condition in which speech is well-ordered, its own kind of fitness, parallel to the virtues of city, body, and soul rather than a report's match to a fact.

Thucydides: speaking with alḗtheia when the city is at risk (classical)

In Nicias' warning to the Syracusans, the speaker names alḗtheia as what his account will aim at even when it seems incredible:

Thucydides, Histories, Eulogikon: dny-aa, ref. 6.33

Ἄπιστα μὲν ἴσως, ὥσπερ καὶ ἄλλοι τινές, δόξω ὑμῖν περὶ τοῦ ἐπίπλου τῆς ἀληθείας λέγειν, καὶ γιγνώσκω ὅτι οἱ τὰ μὴ πιστὰ δοκοῦντα εἶναι ἢ λέγοντες ἢ ἀπαγγέλλοντες οὐ μόνον οὐ πείθουσιν, ἀλλὰ καὶ ἄφρονες δοκοῦσιν εἶναι· ὅμως δὲ οὐ καταφοβηθεὶς ἐπισχήσω κινδυνευούσης τῆς πόλεως.

Ápista mèn ísōs, hṓsper kaì álloi tinés, dóxō hymîn perì toû epíplou tês alētheías légein, kaì gignṓskō hóti hoi tà mḕ pistà dokoûnta eînai ḕ légontes ḕ apangélontes ou mónon ou peíthousin, allà kaì áphrones dokoûsin eînai; hómōs dè ou kataphobētheìs epischḗsō kindyneuoúsēs tês póleōs.

Perhaps I will seem to you, as others do, to be speaking with alḗtheia about the expedition, and I know that those who tell or report what seems not credible not only fail to persuade but seem foolish; nevertheless, not frightened off, I will persist while the city is at risk.

The phrase περὶ τοῦ ἐπίπλου τῆς ἀληθείας λέγειν/perì toû epíplou tês alētheías légein sets speech, the expedition, and alḗtheia together at a moment of peril. The speaker knows the account may sound incredible, yet persists because the city is endangered. Alḗtheia is not a property checked from outside the speech; it is what the speaker commits to under pressure, weighed against seeming foolishness and disbelief.

The dialogue: alḗtheia escapes the unready (classical)

In the Parmenides, the elder Parmenides warns the young Socrates that without long training in argument the thing he is chasing will get away from him:

Plato of Athens, Parmenides, Eulogikon: ffk-ak, ref. Parm.137.c

εἰ δὲ μή, σὲ διαφεύξεται ἡ ἀλήθεια.

ei dè mḗ, sè diapheúxetai hē alḗtheia.

but if not, alḗtheia will get clean away from you.

The verb is διαφεύξεται/diapheúxetai, will flee, will escape, the word used of a quarry that outruns the hunter or a defendant who gets off. Alḗtheia is the subject: it is something that can elude a person who is not equal to it, that slips the grasp of the untrained. It is not a fixed object waiting to be matched against a statement; it moves, and it can outrun you. The reading meets the Sophoclean σθένος/sthénos from the other side: a thing with force enough to prevail is also a thing quick enough to escape.

The treatise: alḗtheia as what inquiry comes to rest in (classical)

Aristotle, opening the discussion of first philosophy, names alḗtheia as the end at which one kind of inquiry arrives:

Aristotle, Metaphysics, Eulogikon: hgw-bj, ref. Metaph.993b

θεωρητικῆς μὲν γὰρ τέλος ἀλήθεια πρακτικῆς δ’ ἔργον·

theōrētikês mèn gàr télos alḗtheia praktikês d’ érgon;

for of contemplative inquiry the télos is alḗtheia, but of practical inquiry a deed.

τέλος/télos is the completion, the point a process arrives at and comes to rest in; alḗtheia is set parallel to ἔργον/érgon, the deed that practical inquiry issues in. So alḗtheia is what contemplative inquiry produces and terminates in, as action terminates in a deed done. It is an end reached, a coming-to-rest of a movement, not a static match standing ready to be checked. The station holds the same active shape as the rest: a place inquiry gets to, over against the road it travels, echoing the road and the light the tragedians gave the word.

The Stoic definition: alḗtheia is a body (Hellenistic)

The most distinctive station is the Stoa, reported in the doxographical tradition. The Stoics separated alḗtheia from its neuter cognate τὸ ἀληθές/to alēthés, the single thing-said, along several axes; on the first, the two differ in substance:

Chrysippus of Soli, Fragments Logical and Physical, Eulogikon: kms-ac, ref. 132

ἡ μὲν ἀλήθεια σῶμά ἐστι, τὸ δὲ ἀληθὲς ἀσώματον ὑπῆρχεν … ἡ ἀλήθεια σῶμά ἐστι παρόσον ἐπιστήμη πάντων ἀληθῶν ἀποφαντικὴ δοκεῖ τυγχάνειν.

hē mèn alḗtheia sō̂má esti, tò dè alēthès asṓmaton hypē̂rchen … hē alḗtheia sō̂má esti paróson epistḗmē pántōn alēthō̂n apophantikḕ dokeî tynchánein.

alḗtheia is a body, but alēthés is bodiless … alḗtheia is a body insofar as it is held to be the knowledge that declares everything it declares to be so.

The predicate is σῶμά ἐστι/sō̂má esti, is a body. The distinction runs along a chain: alḗtheia is knowledge (ἐπιστήμη/epistḗmē); knowledge is a state of the ruling part (ἡγεμονικόν/hēgemonikón); the ruling part, for the Stoics, is a body; so alḗtheia is a body, while alēthés, a single bodiless sayable (λεκτόν/lektón), is not. The reference has moved. In Homer and the court alḗtheia was what a speaker laid open or a case had; here it is the constitution of the knowing thing itself. The word has stepped out of speech and into the body of the knower.

In things themselves: alḗtheia immanent (Hellenistic)

The Epicurean Polystratus, in a papyrus surviving in fragments, sets alḗtheia not in speech or in a knower but in the things that are, and makes grasping it the thing that steadies a person:

Polystratus the Epicurean, On the Contempt of the Irrational, Eulogikon: jss-aa, ref. col20b

πῶς ἡ ἐν τοῖς οὖσιν ἀλήθεια ἔχει, κατανοήσασιν. αὕτη γὰρ οὔτε θρασύνεσθαι παρὰ τὸ προσῆκον οὐθὲν ποιήσει … ἀλλ’ ἀθόρυβον ἀληθινῶς καὶ ἀδεῆ ἐν πᾶσιν εἶναι.

pō̂s hē en toîs oûsin alḗtheia échei, katanoḗsasin. haútē gàr oúte thrasýnesthai parà tò prosē̂kon outhèn poiḗsei … all’ athórybon alēthinō̂s kaì adeē̂ en pâsin eînai.

for those who have grasped how the alḗtheia in the things that are stands. For it will make one neither rash beyond what is fitting … but unshaken, in the full sense, and unafraid in everything.

The phrase is ἡ ἐν τοῖς οὖσιν ἀλήθεια/hē en toîs oûsin alḗtheia, the alḗtheia in the things that are. It is seated in the things themselves, and it does work on the one who grasps it: it steadies them, empties them of dread. Here alḗtheia is neither a feature of statements nor a state of the knower but something in what is, from which a settling force runs back into the person who takes it in.

Needing nothing from outside: alḗtheia self-sufficient (Hellenistic)

Philo, reporting a magistrate's words, closes a sentence with a maxim in which alḗtheia stands as its own warrant:

Philo of Alexandria, Against Flaccus, Eulogikon: lgi-bf, ref. Flac 98

ἡ γὰρ ἀλήθεια αὐταρκέστατος ἔπαινος.

hē gàr alḗtheia autarkéstatos épainos.

for alḗtheia is the most self-sufficient commendation.

The superlative αὐταρκέστατος/autarkéstatos, most self-sufficient, is from αὐτάρκης/autárkēs, the self-standing that needs nothing added from without. Alḗtheia requires no external endorsement to carry weight; it commends by standing on its own. This meets the Polystratus reading from the other side: there alḗtheia is seated in things and steadies the one who grasps it, here it needs nothing from anyone to hold. In both, the word names something with its force in itself.

What alḗtheia was like

Across nine centuries and five kinds of writing the word does not sit still on a single English word, and it does not behave like a correspondence between a statement and the world checked from outside. At the origin, in Homer, alḗtheia is what a speaker lays fully open in speech, the opposite of playing false or holding back. From there it spreads without losing that center. Heraclitus has one speak and act it by nature; Empedocles makes it a meadow the soul presses toward, away from ruin; the tragedians give it strength that can prevail or be overpowered, and figure it as light against night and as an open road against stealth; Herodotus sets it against flattery as the thing one deals in; the physicians use it to mark the real state of a body under a misleading surface; the orators send it into the battle line as a fighting ally, and make it the good order proper to speech; Thucydides sets it in speech under peril, when the account may seem incredible; Plato has it flee the inquirer who is not ready for it; Aristotle names it the end that contemplative inquiry comes to rest in; and the Hellenistic schools carry it furthest, seating it in the things that are, making it self-sufficient, and at the limit calling it a body.

What holds these together is not a shared translation but a shared shape: alḗtheia is the standing-open of what might have stayed hidden, and wherever it appears it acts. It prevails and is prevailed over, it fights, it lights, it steadies, it presses toward and is pressed toward, it flees the unready and is the end the ready arrive at, it is furnished by deeds and seated in things, and at the end it is the very body of the knower. To reach for the word was to reach for the condition of a thing brought out from under concealment, and for a power at work in bringing it out. The English truth, as a match inspected from outside, catches none of that. The word does not reduce to it; it holds together a range the receiving languages had to break apart.

Caveats and open questions

  • English gloss. A common gloss is truth as propositional correctness. The passages above track active verbs and contrasts; they do not settle a single philosophical definition.
  • Fragment witnesses. The Aeschylus line (emo-ah, book 176) is also transmitted with Stobaean attribution to the Judgment of Arms at emo-ag, book 288.29. The Euripides road-line (cbm-am, book 289.2) survives as a fragmentary unit.
  • Thucydides 6.33. Nicias' phrase περὶ τοῦ ἐπίπλου τῆς ἀληθείας λέγειν is often read as "to speak the truth about the expedition." The genitive τῆς ἀληθείας is present in the corpus witness; the exact syntactic force remains disputed.
  • Stoic report. Chrysippus' definition of alḗtheia as a body (kms-ac, 132) is doxographical, not a verbatim treatise excerpt.
