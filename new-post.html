Pneûma: wind, breath, and tension

Τεκμήρια · Eulogikon · 2 July 2026 · pneûma from Anaximenes to Galen

Pneûma begins as wind and breath. In the early evidence it names cosmic air and the upward-moving element at death. Across Greek philosophy and medicine it then becomes the vehicle of bodily and cognitive function, a debated definition in Aristotle, and the Stoic medium of cohesion, life, perception, and movement. The passages below trace that line from Anaximenes to Galen.

Corpus frequency: 14,604 total attestations. The period counts below belong to the Eulogikon corpus at the time of composition; they show both the survival pattern of Greek literature and the increasing technicalization of pneûma in medicine, Stoicism, and later Christian writing.

Anaximenes: pneûma, air, and psychē

Anaximenes equates pneûma with aēr and psychē in a cosmological frame:

Anaximenes of Miletus, Fragments on Cold and Heat, Eulogikon: bcm-ab, ref. frag. 2

ἡ ψυχὴ ἡ ἡμετέρα ἀὴρ οὖσα συγκρατεῖ ἡμᾶς, καὶ ὅλον τὸν κόσμον πνεῦμα καὶ ἀὴρ περιέχει (λέγεται δὲ συνωνύμως ἀὴρ καὶ πνεῦμα)

hē psychē hē hēmetéra aēr oûsa synkrateî hēmâs, kaì hólon tòn kósmon pneûma kaì aēr periéchei (légetai dè synōnýmōs aēr kaì pneûma)

Our psychē, being air, holds us together, and the whole cosmos is encompassed by pneûma and air (air and pneûma are said synonymously).

Anaximenes places pneûma, aēr, and psychē in the same physical field. The fragment uses pneûma and aēr synonymously, and describes this air as what holds both the person and the cosmos together.

Epicharmus: pneûma upward

A fragment on death preserves another early use:

Epicharmus of Syracuse, Gnomic and Comic Wisdom Fragments, Eulogikon: bhm-ag, ref. frag. 9

συνεκρίθη καὶ διεκρίθη κἀπῆλθεν ὅθεν ἦλθεν πάλιν, γᾶ μὲν εἰς γᾶν, πνεῦμα δ' ἄνω

synekríthē kaì diekríthē kapêlthen hóthen êlthen pálin, gâ mèn eis gân, pneûma d' ánō

It was compounded and separated and returned whence it came: earth to earth, pneûma upward.

The contrast is already visible. Earth returns downward; pneûma rises. The distinction remains physical rather than theological, but it introduces an opposition later philosophers repeatedly revisit.

Hippocratic medicine: breath and cognition

The Hippocratic writers use pneûma in physiological contexts: embryonic heat, voice, respiration, movement, and understanding.

Hippocrates and the Hippocratic Corpus, On the Nature of the Child, Eulogikon: bww-ad, ref. 12.1

Ἔπειτα πνεῦμα ἴσχει, ἅτε ἐν θερμῷ ἐοῦσα... τὸ δὲ πνεῦμα ῥήγνυσι καὶ ποιέει οἱ ὁδὸν αὐτὸ ἑωυτῷ καὶ χωρέει ἔξω

épeita pneûma íschei, háte en thermō̂i eoûsa... tò dè pneûma rhḗgnysi kaì poiéei hoi hodòn autò heōutō̂i kaì chōréei éxō

Then it gets pneûma, since it is in a warm place... and the pneûma breaks through and makes a path for itself and moves outward.

Hippocrates and the Hippocratic Corpus, On Meats, Eulogikon: bww-bz, ref. 18

οἱ ἄνθρωποι ὁκόταν βούλωνται μέγα φωνῆσαι, ἕλκοντες τὸ πνεῦμα τὸ ἔξω ὠθέουσι θύραζε καὶ φθέγγονται μέγα ὡς ἀντηχῇ τὸ πνεῦμα

hoi ánthrōpoi hokótan boúlōntai méga phōnêsai, hélkontes tò pneûma tò éxō ōthéousi thýraze kaì phthéngontai méga hōs antēchêi tò pneûma

When people wish to shout loudly, drawing the pneûma, they push it outward and speak loudly so that the pneûma resounds.

Hippocrates and the Hippocratic Corpus, On the Sacred Disease, Eulogikon: bww-ao, ref. 7

ὅταν γὰρ λάβῃ ἄνθρωπος κατὰ τὸ στόμα καὶ τοὺς μυκτῆρας τὸ πνεῦμα, πρῶτον μὲν ἐς τὸν ἐγκέφαλον ἔρχεται, ἔπειτα δὲ ἐς τὴν κοιλίην τὸ πλεῖστον μέρος, τὸ δὲ ἐπὶ τὸν πλεύμονα, τὸ δὲ ἐπὶ τὰς φλέβας. Ἐκ τουτέων δὲ σκίδναται ἐς τὰ λοιπὰ μέρεα κατὰ τὰς φλέβας... ὁ δ' ἐς τὸν πλεύμονά τε καὶ τὰς φλέβας ἀὴρ ξυμβάλλεται ἐς τὰς κοιλίας ἐσιὼν καὶ ἐς τὸν ἐγκέφαλον, καὶ οὕτω τὴν φρόνησιν καὶ τὴν κίνησιν τοῖσι μέλεσι παρέχει

hótan gàr lábēi ánthrōpos katà tò stóma kaì toùs myktêras tò pneûma, prôton mèn es tòn enképhalon érchetai, épeita dè es tḕn koilíēn tò pleîston méros, tò dè epì tòn pleúmona, tò dè epì tàs phlébas. Ek toutéōn dè skídnatai es tà loipà mérea katà tàs phlébas... ho d' es tòn pleúmoná te kaì tàs phlébas aēr xymbálletai es tàs koilías esiṑn kaì es tòn enképhalon, kaì hoútō tḕn phrónēsin kaì tḕn kínēsin toîsi mélesi paréchei

For when a person takes pneûma through the mouth and nostrils, first it goes to the brain, then mostly to the belly, some to the lungs, some to the veins. From these it is dispersed to the remaining parts through the veins... and the air that goes to the lungs and veins contributes to the cavities and to the brain, and thus provides understanding and movement to the limbs.

In these medical passages, pneûma is physiological: embryonic heat, vocal force, respiratory movement, and air moving through the body. The passage from On the Sacred Disease gives it cognitive consequence: air and pneûma reach the brain and provide phrónēsis and bodily movement.

Aristotle: pneûma as moved air

Aristotle separates pneûma from aēr by treating it as moving air rather than air as such:

Aristotle, Topics, Eulogikon: hgw-cd, ref. Top. 127a

τὸ πνεῦμα ἀέρα κινούμενον. μᾶλλον γὰρ κίνησις ἀέρος τὸ πνεῦμα... ὥστ' οὐκ ἔστιν ὅλως ἀὴρ τὸ πνεῦμα

tò pneûma aéra kinoúmenon. mâllon gàr kínēsis aéros tò pneûma... hṓst' ouk éstin hólōs aēr tò pneûma

Pneûma is air being moved. For pneûma is rather a movement of air... so pneûma is not air simply.

Aristotle's definition changes the relation between the terms. Pneûma is not simply aēr; it is aēr under motion. The same air may be still or moving, but only moving air is called pneûma.

The Stoics: heat, function, and tension

Zeno identifies pneûma with heat, as reported by Rufus of Ephesus:

Rufus of Ephesus, On the Terms of the Human Body, Eulogikon: ojc-ab, ref. 228

Θερμασίαν δὲ καὶ πνεῦμα Ζήνων μὲν τὸ αὐτὸ εἶναί φησιν

thermasían dè kaì pneûma Zḗnōn mèn tò autò eînaí phēsin

Zeno says that heat and pneûma are the same thing.

Chrysippus makes pneûma the medium of psychic function. Vision and hearing are described as pneûma stretched from the ruling part to the organs:

Chrysippus of Soli, Fragments Logical and Physical, Eulogikon: kms-ac, ref. frag. 836

ἡ μὲν ὅρασις ἐστὶ πνεῦμα διατεῖνον ἀπὸ ἡγεμονικοῦ μέχρις ὀφθαλμῶν, ἀκοὴ δὲ πνεῦμα διατεῖνον ἀπὸ τοῦ ἡγεμονικοῦ μέχρις ὤτων... αὐτὸ δὲ τὸ ἡγεμονικόν... ἐν τῇ ἡμετέρᾳ σφαιροειδεῖ κεφαλῇ

hē mèn hórasis estì pneûma diateînon apò hēgemonikoû méchris ophthalmôn, akoḕ dè pneûma diateînon apò toû hēgemonikoû méchris ōtōn... autò dè tò hēgemonikón... en tē̂i hēmetérāi sphairoeideî kephalē̂i

Vision is pneûma stretching from the ruling part to the eyes, and hearing is pneûma stretching from the ruling part to the ears... and the ruling part itself is in our spherical head.

The Stoic fragments also distinguish three grades of pneûma:

Chrysippus of Soli, Fragments Logical and Physical, Eulogikon: kms-ac, ref. frag. 716

ἑκτικὸν μὲν οὖν ἐστι πνεῦμα τὸ συνέχον τοὺς λίθους, φυσικὸν δὲ τὸ τρέφον τὰ ζῷα καὶ τὰ φυτά, ψυχικὸν δὲ τὸ ἐπὶ τῶν ἐμψύχων αἰσθητικά τε ποιοῦν τὰ ζῷα καὶ κινούμενα πᾶσαν κίνησιν

hektikòn mèn oûn esti pneûma tò synéchon toùs líthous, physikòn dè tò tréphon tà zō̂ia kaì tà phytá, psychikòn dè tò epì tō̂n empsýchōn aisthētiká te poioûn tà zō̂ia kaì kinoúmena pâsan kínēsin

Cohesive pneûma is what holds stones together; natural pneûma is what nourishes animals and plants; psychic pneûma is what, in ensouled beings, makes animals capable of perception and moves them in every movement.

The coherence of bodies is described through tónos, the tension of pneûma:

Chrysippus of Soli, Fragments Logical and Physical, Eulogikon: kms-ac, ref. frag. 441

τίς γὰρ καὶ ὁ τόνος τοῦ πνεύματος, ὑφ' οὗ συνδούμενα τήν τε συνέχειαν ἔχει τὰ οἰκεῖα μέρη καὶ συνῆπται τοῖς παρακειμένοις;

tís gàr kaì ho tónos toû pneúmatos, hyph' hoû syndoúmena tḗn te synécheian échei tà oikeîa mérē kaì synêptai toîs parakeiménois?

For what is the tension of the pneûma, by which the proper parts are bound together, have coherence, and are joined to their neighbors?

Compared with Aristotle, the Stoic texts restore pneûma to the role of active principle and distinguish grades organized by function: hektikón for cohesion, physikón for growth and nourishment, and psychikón for perception and movement. The account of tónos describes that principle as tension within bodies.

Galen: medical pneûma retained

Galen keeps the medical vocabulary of pneûma in contexts of respiration and diagnostic description:

Galen of Pergamon, On Difficulty of Breathing, Eulogikon: qmm-ck, ref. 7.845

πνεῦμα ἀραιὸν, μέγα, διὰ χρόνου

pneûma araiòn, méga, dià chrónou

Pneûma: rare, large, at intervals.

Galen of Pergamon, On Difficulty of Breathing, Eulogikon: qmm-ck, ref. 7.910

μικρὸν καὶ πυκνὸν γενέσθαι τὸ πνεῦμα

mikròn kaì pyknòn genésthai tò pneûma

The pneûma becomes small and frequent.

Galen preserves the medical usage of pneûma while writing in a world where Stoic and theological meanings already exist. His diagnostic vocabulary remains physiological rather than metaphysical: rare or dense, large or small, intermittent or frequent.

The diachronic shape

Period Count Character
Archaic 66 Cosmic air, breath, upward movement at death
Classical 717 Medical physiology, meteorology, Aristotelian definition
Hellenistic 889 Stoic technicalization: cohesion, growth, perception, movement
Roman 3,013 Medical synthesis and philosophical commentary
Late Antique 3,979 Theological expansion beyond the scope of this survey

The dramatic increase in attestations after the Hellenistic period reflects both the expansion of surviving literature and the increasing technicalization of pneûma in medicine, Stoicism, and later Christian writing.

On the evidence

  • In Anaximenes, pneûma, aēr, and psychē are explicitly brought into synonymy.
  • In Epicharmus, earth returns downward and pneûma rises upward. The contrast is physical, not yet theological.
  • In the Hippocratic passages, pneûma remains physiological: breath, heat, voice, bodily movement, and air reaching the brain.
  • Aristotle distinguishes pneûma from aēr: pneûma is moved air, not air simply.
  • The Stoic fragments restore pneûma as an active principle of cohesion, growth, perception, and movement.
  • Medical writers retain the physiological sense throughout. Later theological writers inherit these earlier layers rather than replacing them.
