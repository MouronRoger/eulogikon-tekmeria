παράκλητος

paráklētos

the one called to your side

Τεκμήρια · Eulogikon · 25 June 2026 · 81 attestations across six centuries

Paráklētos is a verbal adjective in -tos from parakaléō (pará + kaléō): called to one’s side. The one called comes to help — to plead, to speak for, to intercede. In the Eulogikon corpus the word appears 81 times across six periods, but its center of gravity is late: 63 occurrences are late-antique Christian theology (Origen, Eusebius, Socrates Scholasticus, Theodoret). Before that, three classical uses — and nothing in between, then a brief Hellenistic bloom in Philo, then the theological explosion.

The three classical attestations are the anchor. They all mean the same thing: a person summoned to plead another’s case.

Classical: the law-court (c. 340–270 BCE)

Demosthenes, prosecuting Aeschines, opens by contrasting public interest with private lobbying.

Demosthenes, On the Embassy, Eulogikon: fsm-cc, ref. Or19 1

αἱ δὲ τῶν παρακλήτων αὗται δεήσεις καὶ σπουδαὶ τῶν ἰδίων πλεονεξιῶν εἵνεκα γίγνονται

hai de tōn paraklētōn hautai deēseis kai spoudai tōn idiōn pleonexiōn heineka gignontai

But these entreaties and efforts of the advocates happen for the sake of private advantage.

Tōn paraklētōn is a subjective genitive: the entreaties that the advocates make. The word is a forensic noun, already nominalised — one called in to speak for another in a legal-political setting.

Lycurgus of Athens, preserved only in a lexicographical fragment (s.v.):

Lycurgus of Athens, Defense Speeches, Eulogikon: hfi-ab, ref. Frag 15 13

s. v. παράκλητος · ὡς ἡμεῖς εἴρηκεν Λυκοῦργος.

s.v. paráklētos: hōs hēmeis eirēken Lykourgos.

Entry: paráklētos — as Lycurgus said.

The 9th-century lexicographer Photius (Eulogikon: woy-an, ref. pi 385) adds the gloss that Lycurgus used paráklētos in place of deēsis (entreaty). For the classical orator the word meant one summoned to plead, a forensic intercessor.

Bion of Borysthenes, the Cynic, used the word in a retort. Asked by a persistent man to intercede for him, Bion replied:

Bion of Borysthenes, Philosophical Autobiography, Eulogikon: hks-aa, ref. 74 (n)

τὸ ἱκανόν σοι ποιήσω, ἐὰν παρακλήτους πέμψῃς καὶ αὐτὸς μὴ ἔλθῃς

to hikanón soi poiēsō, eàn paraklētous pémpsēs kai autòs mē élthēs

I will satisfy you, if you send advocates and do not come yourself.

Send advocates to plead your case — do not come yourself. The same forensic sense. Three uses, three authors, spanning roughly 340–270 BCE. Oratory and Cynic philosophy. A living word in Athenian forensic life, but not a common one. The same sentence appears in Diogenes Laertius (Eulogikon: rjo-ad, ref. Vit.4.50).

Then a gap. The word vanishes from surviving Greek for 250 years.

Hellenistic and Roman: Philo of Alexandria (1st c. CE)

Philo of Alexandria brings it back, still secular. In Against Flaccus:

Philo, Against Flaccus, Eulogikon: lgi-bf, ref. Flac 21

δεῖ δὴ παράκλητον ἡμᾶς εὑρεῖν δυνατώτατον, ὑφ' οὗ Γάιος ἐξευμενισθήσεται

dei dē paráklēton hēmas heurein dynatōtaton, hyph' hou Gáios exeumenisthēsetai

We must find a most powerful advocate through whom Gaius may be appeased.

The paráklētos is a political intercessor — someone with access to power who speaks for those who do not. Two sentences later in the same passage, Alexandria itself is named as paráklētos before the emperor (Flac 23). In On the Creation of the World, Philo personifies Peithō (Persuasion) as the senses’ paráklētos before the ruling faculty. The word is still forensic, still intercessory, but now it can be an abstract force.

Philo, On the Creation of the World, Eulogikon: lgi-aw, ref. Opif 165

δεξάμεναι τὰ δῶρα θεραπαινίδων τρόπον προσφέρουσιν οἷα δεσπότῃ τῷ λογισμῷ, παράκλητον ἐπαγόμεναι πειθὼ περὶ τοῦ μηδὲν ἀπώσασθαι τὸ παράπαν

dexámenai tà dōra therapainídōn trópon prosphérousin hoia despótē tō logismō, paráklēton epagómenai peithō perì tou mēdèn apōsasthai tò parápan

Having received the gifts, they bring them like handmaids to the ruling faculty as to a master, bringing in persuasion as one called to the side that he reject nothing at all.

Roman: the Valentinian aeon (2nd–3rd c. CE)

The pivot comes in the 2nd–3rd c. CE with Valentinian Gnosticism, transmitted through Clement of Alexandria:

Clement of Alexandria, Excerpts from Theodotus, Eulogikon: qya-ae, ref. 1.23

Τὸν Παράκλητον οἱ ἀπὸ Οὐαλεντίνου τὸν Ἰησοῦν λέγουσιν… Ἰησοῦς προβάλλεται παράκλητος τῷ παρελθόντι αἰῶνι

Tòn Paráklēton hoi apò Oualentínou tòn Iēsoun légousin… Iēsous probálletai paráklētos tō parelthónti aiōni

The Valentinians call Jesus the Paraclete… Jesus is projected forth as paraclete for the aeon that had passed away.

Paráklētos has crossed into cosmology. It is now an aeon — a divine entity interceding across the Pleroma. The forensic metaphor remains (one who pleads for another), but the domain has shifted from the law-court to the intra-divine. In Hippolytus’ account of the Valentinian system, Paráklētos is one of the names assigned to the twelve aeons, listed alongside Pístis (faith), Elpís (hope), and Agápē (love):

Hippolytus, Refutation of All Heresies, Eulogikon: qsg-at, ref. 6.30.5

οἷς ταῦτα τὰ ὀνόματα χαρίζονται· Παράκλητος καὶ Πίστις, Πατρικὸς καὶ Ἐλπίς, Μητρικὸς καὶ Ἀγάπη

hois tauta tà onómata charízontai: Paráklētos kai Pístis, Patrikòs kai Elpís, Mētrikòs kai Agápē

to whom they assign these names: Paraclete and Faith, Patricius and Hope, Matricius and Love.

Late-antique: the Holy Spirit (3rd–5th c. CE)

The great concentration (63 of 81 occurrences) is late-antique Christian theology, where paráklētos becomes the standard Greek term for the Holy Spirit, grounded in John 14–16 and 1 John 2:1. Origen cites 1 John 2:1:

Origen, Commentary on the Gospel of John, Eulogikon: uhs-aw, ref. 1.240

ὁ παράκλητος ἐν τῇ Ἰωάννου λεγόμενος ἐπιστολῇ· »Ἐὰν γάρ τις ἁμάρτῃ, παράκλητον ἔχομεν πρὸς τὸν πατέρα Ἰησοῦν Χριστὸν δίκαιον«

ho paráklētos en tē Iōánnou legoménē epistolē: "Eàn gár tis hamártē, paráklēton échomen pròs tòn patéra Iēsoun Christòn díkaion"

The paraclete spoken of in John’s letter: ‘If anyone sins, we have a paraclete with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous.’

Eusebius explicitly distinguishes Christ from “another paraclete,” the Holy Spirit:

Eusebius, On Ecclesiastical Theology, Eulogikon: tva-at, ref. 3.5.6

ἐγὼ ἐρωτήσω τὸν πατέρα καὶ ἄλλον παράκλητον δώσει ὑμῖν… οὐκοῦν ἕτερος ἦν παρ' αὐτὸν ὁ παράκλητος

egō erōtēsō tòn patéra kai állon paráklēton dōsei hymin… oukoun héteros ēn par' autòn ho paráklētos

‘I will ask the Father and he will give you another paraclete’… so the paraclete was another beside him.

Socrates Scholasticus equates them in a creed:

Socrates Scholasticus, Ecclesiastical History, Eulogikon: tga-aa, ref. HE 2 30

Ὁ δὲ Παράκλητος, τὸ Πνεῦμα τὸ Ἅγιον δι' Υἱοῦ ἀποσταλέν, ἦλθε κατὰ τὴν ἐπαγγελίαν

Ho dè Paráklētos, tò Pneuma tò Hágion di' Hyiou apostalén, ēlthe katà tēn epangelían

The Paraclete, the Holy Spirit sent through the Son, came according to the promise.

Nonnus of Panopolis composes the Paraphrase of John in Homeric hexameters, bending the theological term into dactyls:

Nonnus, Paraphrase of the Gospel of John, Eulogikon: tns-aa, ref. book 14

παράκλητος δ' ὅταν ἔλθῃ, ὑμέας ἰθυντῆρι λόγῳ ξύμπαντα διδάξει

paráklētos d' hótan élthē, hyméas ithyntēri lógō xýmpanta didáxei

And when the paraclete comes, he will teach you all things with a guiding word.

The trajectory is a single semantic arc, not a series of ruptures. At every stage paráklētos retains the shape of its root: one called to the side to speak for another. What changes is the domain. The Athenian jury-court → the Hellenistic royal audience → the Gnostic Pleroma → the Christian Trinity. The word stays constant; its world expands.

Pará “to the side” + kaléō “call” + -tos (verbal adjective of potential/result). The word never loses the trace of its making.

On the evidence

  • John 14–16 and 1 John 2:1, where the word is used of the Spirit and of Christ, are not in the Eulogikon corpus. The late-antique attestations above quote or gloss those verses; the verses themselves are not present.
  • The corpus holds no attestation of paráklētos between Bion (c. 270 BCE) and Philo (1st c. CE). The classical and the Philonic uses are separated by this interval in the surviving record.
  • The Bion sentence is transmitted by Diogenes Laertius, c. 3rd c. CE, some five centuries after Bion.
  • The Lycurgus attestation is lexicographical in form (s.v.), not a continuous speech. The same gloss appears in Photius (woy-an, ref. pi 385).
  • 63 of the 81 attestations are late-antique Christian theological texts. The remaining 18 are distributed: 3 classical, 7 Hellenistic, 6 Roman, 3 Byzantine (with 1 archaic-dated, a 1st-c. CE text dated early in the database).
