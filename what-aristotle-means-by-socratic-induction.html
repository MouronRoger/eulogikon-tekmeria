What Aristotle means by attributing inductive arguments and universal definitions to Socrates

Σημεῖα — Eulogikon · 24 June 2026 · on Metaphysics 1078b17–31

The passage (Metaphysics 1078b17–31)

Aristotle, in his history of the theory of Forms (εἴδη / ἰδέαι), writes:

Σωκράτους δὲ περὶ τὰς ἠθικὰς ἀρετὰς πραγματευομένου καὶ περὶ τούτων ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου ζητοῦντος πρώτου… δύο γάρ ἐστιν ἅ τις ἂν ἀποδοίη Σωκράτει δικαίως, τούς τ' ἐπακτικοὺς λόγους καὶ τὸ ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου· ταῦτα γάρ ἐστιν ἄμφω περὶ ἀρχὴν ἐπιστήμης.

“Socrates occupied himself with the ethical virtues and was the first to seek to define them universally… For there are two things one could justly attribute to Socrates: inductive arguments (τούς τ' ἐπακτικοὺς λόγους) and universal definition (τὸ ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου). For both of these concern the starting-point of science.”

(Aristotle, Metaphysics, Eulogikon: hgw-bj, ref. Metaph.1078b)

1. What are “inductive arguments” (ἐπακτικοὶ λόγοι)?

The term ἐπακτικοὶ λόγοι means “arguments that proceed by induction” — reasoning that moves from particular cases to a general conclusion. Aristotle himself explains the method in the Topics (Eulogikon: hgw-cd, ref. Top.108b):

Ἡ δὲ τοῦ ὁμοίου θεωρία χρήσιμος πρός τε τοὺς ἐπακτικοὺς λόγους… πρὸς μὲν οὖν τοὺς ἐπακτικοὺς λόγους, διότι τῇ καθ' ἕκαστα ἐπὶ τῶν ὁμοίων ἐπαγωγῇ τὸ καθόλου ἀξιοῦμεν ἐπάγειν· οὐ γὰρ ῥᾴδιόν ἐστιν ἐπάγειν μὴ εἰδότας τὰ ὅμοια.

“The study of likeness is useful for inductive arguments… For inductive arguments, because by induction of the particular cases among similars we claim to bring in the universal; for it is not easy to induce without knowing the similars.”

So an ἐπακτικὸς λόγος is a dialectical procedure: you gather several similar particular cases (e.g., “courage in battle is good,” “courage in facing illness is good,” “courage in facing poverty is good”), and from these you infer the universal claim (“courage is good”). Socrates is famous for doing exactly this in Plato's early dialogues — asking “What is X?” by collecting examples and testing whether a proposed definition fits them all.

Alexander of Aphrodisias (Eulogikon: reo-ae, ref. in Metaph 741) clarifies why this matters for Aristotle's story:

ἀρχὴ γὰρ συλλογισμοῦ καὶ ἡ ἐπαγωγὴ καὶ ὁ ὁρισμός.

“For both induction and definition are a starting-point of syllogism.”

Induction (ἐπαγωγή) is one of the two fundamental ways to reach the premises from which deduction (συλλογισμός) proceeds. Socrates, lacking the developed dialectical technique that would later allow arguing from opposed positions without definitions, needed definitions as fixed starting-points.

2. What is “universal definition” (τὸ ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου)?

τὸ ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου means “defining universally” — i.e., giving a definition that captures what something is in every case, not just in this or that instance. Aristotle says Socrates was the first (πρώτου) to seek this systematically, and he did so specifically in the domain of ethics (περὶ τὰς ἠθικὰς ἀρετάς).

The key contrast is between asking “What is this particular beautiful thing?” (τόδε τὸ καλόν) and asking “What is the beautiful itself, universally?” (τί τὸ καλόν ἐστι καθόλου). As Asclepius (Eulogikon: ave-ab, ref. In met.45) explains:

Σωκράτους δὲ περὶ μὲν τὰ ἠθικὰ πραγματευσαμένου καὶ ἐν τούτοις τὸ καθόλου ζητοῦντος — διαλαμβάνων γὰρ περὶ καλοῦ ἤ τινος ἄλλου ἐζήτει καθόλου τί τὸ καλόν ἐστιν, οὐ τόδε τὸ καλὸν λαμβάνων — καὶ ὁρίζεσθαι τὸ προκείμενον ἀεὶ πειρωμένου.

“Socrates occupied himself with ethical matters and in these sought the universal — for when discussing the beautiful or something else, he sought universally what the beautiful is, not taking this particular beautiful thing — and always tried to define the subject at hand.”

3. Why these two together? The epistemological point

Aristotle says both are περὶ ἀρχὴν ἐπιστήμης — “concerning the starting-point of science/knowledge.” Here is why:

  • Induction (ἐπαγωγή) is the method by which we arrive at universal premises from particular observations. It is the movement upward from many cases to one general claim.
  • Definition (ὁρισμός) is the expression of that universal — what the thing is (τὸ τί ἦν εἶναι). It is the fixed starting-point from which deduction can then proceed.

Together they form the two poles of scientific method as Aristotle conceives it: induction supplies the universal premise, definition articulates it, and deduction (syllogism) draws conclusions from it. Socrates, in Aristotle's view, originated both procedures — but only in the ethical domain, and without separating the universal from the particulars.

4. The crucial caveat: Socrates vs. Plato

Aristotle immediately adds the decisive distinction (Metaph. 1078b30–31):

ἀλλ' ὁ μὲν Σωκράτης τὰ καθόλου οὐ χωριστὰ ἐποίει οὐδὲ τοὺς ὁρισμούς· οἱ δ' ἐχώρισαν, καὶ τὰ τοιαῦτα τῶν ὄντων ἰδέας προσηγόρευσαν.

“But Socrates did not make the universals separate [from particulars], nor the definitions; whereas they [Plato and the Platonists] separated them, and called such things ‘Forms’ (ἰδέαι).”

This is the heart of Aristotle's historical narrative. Socrates' innovation was:

  • Methodological: He introduced inductive argument and the search for universal definitions as a procedure for inquiry.
  • Immanent: He treated the universal as something in the particulars — the common nature shared by all courageous acts, not a separate entity existing apart from them.

Plato then took Socrates' universal definitions and separated them (ἐχώρισαν) from the sensible world, turning them into independently existing Forms (ἰδέαι). This separation — not the search for definitions itself — is what Aristotle criticises.

Alexander of Aphrodisias (Eulogikon: reo-ae, ref. in Metaph 741) makes the same point:

ἀλλ' ὁ μὲν Σωκράτης τὰ καθόλου, ὧν καὶ τοὺς ὁρισμοὺς ἐζήτει, οὐ χωριστὰ ἐποίει τῶν αἰσθητῶν, οὐδὲ τοὺς ὁρισμοὺς χωριστῶν φύσεων εἶναι ἔλεγεν, οἱ δὲ περὶ Πλάτωνα ἐχώρισαν καὶ τὰ τοιαῦτα ἰδέας κατωνόμασαν.

“But Socrates did not make the universals — of which he also sought the definitions — separate from the sensibles, nor did he say the definitions belonged to separate natures; whereas those around Plato separated them and called such things ‘Forms’.”

Summary

What Aristotle attributes to SocratesWhat it means
τούς τ' ἐπακτικοὺς λόγους (inductive arguments)Reasoning from particular cases to a general conclusion — the method of collecting examples to arrive at a universal claim
τὸ ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου (universal definition)Defining what something is in every case, not just describing this or that instance — asking “What is X?” universally
Why both matterThey are the two foundations of scientific knowledge (περὶ ἀρχὴν ἐπιστήμης): induction supplies the universal, definition fixes it as a starting-point for deduction
What Socrates did not doHe did not separate the universal from particulars into a transcendent realm — that was Plato's innovation, which Aristotle rejects

Caveats and open questions

  • The historical accuracy of Aristotle's claim is debated. The Socrates we know from Plato's dialogues does separate the Forms in middle-period works (e.g., Phaedo, Republic). Aristotle may be reading his own methodological categories back onto a historical figure, or distinguishing the early “Socratic” phase of Plato's thought from the later “Platonic” one.
  • The Pythagoreans are mentioned as having given definitions before Socrates (e.g., defining justice as a number), but Aristotle dismisses these as “superficial and unsystematic” (μαλακῶς καὶ ἀμεθόδως, as the commentators note). The innovation is not definition per se, but definition pursued methodically through induction.
  • What the corpus does not show: We have no autograph Socratic writings. All evidence for Socrates' method comes through Plato, Xenophon, and Aristotle himself — so the attribution rests on Aristotle's testimony, not on independent Socratic texts in the corpus.
