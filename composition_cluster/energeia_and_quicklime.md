Answer: Yes — but the pre-Aristotelian uses are rare, mostly doxographic, and never technical
Direct answer
ἐνέργεια does occur before Aristotle, but only in a handful of passages, and every single one is either (a) a doxographer's report attributing the term to an earlier thinker, (b) a later grammatical/rhetorical usage, or (c) a non-technical everyday sense. The term is absent from Plato and from the surviving original texts of the Presocratics. Aristotle is the first author to make ἐνέργεια a load-bearing philosophical term.

The evidence, period by period
1. Archaic period (4 occurrences)
Aesop (fl. -620) — Aesopic Fables (aqo-ap, sid 837649)

ὅπως μὴ χρησάμενοι τῇ τοῦ ἰξοῦ ἐνεργείᾳ συλλαμβάνωσιν αὐτά "so that they, using the activity/operation of the birdlime, should not catch them"

#energeia #finding The Aesop passage uses ἐνέργεια in its ordinary everyday sense: the "working" or "operative power" of birdlime (sticky mistletoe sap used to trap birds). This is not a philosophical term — it means simply "the way something works / its active property."

Xenophanes (fl. -540) — Testimonies (beu-ab, sid 1273739)

TERTULL. de anima c. 43: Anaxagoras cum Xenophane defetiscentiam [gr. κόπον τῆς σωματικῆς ἐνεργείας] "Anaxagoras together with Xenophanes [say that sleep comes from] weariness of bodily activity"

#energeia #testimonia This is Tertullian (a Latin Christian author, 2nd-3rd c. CE) reporting a doxographic claim. The Greek phrase κόπον τῆς σωματικῆς ἐνεργείας is Tertullian's own gloss or his source's wording, not Xenophanes' own. The term is used in a physiological sense: bodily exertion/activity that causes fatigue.

Pythagoras (fl. -530) — Fragments on Sacred Doctrine (bgs-ae, sid 700858)

Πυθαγόρας δὲ (sc. φησὶν τὸν ἀριθμὸν εἶναι) ἔκτασιν καὶ ἐνέργειαν τῶν ἐν μονάδι σπερματικῶν λόγων "Pythagoras says that number is the extension and activity of the spermatic principles in the monad"

#energeia #testimonia This is a late Neopythagorean report (from Nicomachus of Gerasa's Introduction to Arithmetic, via Iamblichus' Theologoumena Arithmeticae). The language — σπερματικῶν λόγων (spermatic principles) — is Stoic terminology, not 6th-century Pythagorean. The passage is a Hellenistic or later retrojection.

Anaximander (fl. -570) — Testimonies (aua-aa, sid 1671949)

οὐ δύναται δὲ ἡ ὕλη εἶναι ἐνέργεια, ἐὰν μὴ τὸ ποιοῦν ὑποκέηται "Matter cannot be activity/actuality unless the efficient cause underlies it"

#energeia #testimonia This is from Aëtius (1st-2nd c. CE), a doxographer. The passage uses the Aristotelian hylomorphic framework (ὕλη / ποιοῦν / ἐνέργεια) to criticise Anaximander. It is a later philosophical commentary, not Anaximander's own thought.

2. Classical period — pre-Aristotelian
Philolaus (fl. -470) — On the Nature of the Cosmos (bue-ab, sid 1679749)

διὸ καὶ καλῶς ἔχειν ἔλεγε, κόσμον ἦμεν ἐνέργειαν ἀίδιον θεῶ τε καὶ γενέσιος κατὰ συνακολουθίαν τᾶς μεταβλατικᾶς φύσιος "Therefore he said it was fitting that the cosmos is an eternal activity of god and of generation according to the sequence of changing physis"

#energeia #open This is the most philosophically interesting pre-Aristotelian use. The fragment is transmitted by Stobaeus (5th c. CE) and written in Doric dialect. It presents the cosmos as an eternal ἐνέργεια — a kinetic, active principle. However, the fragment's authenticity is disputed: many scholars consider the Philolaus fragments to be Hellenistic forgeries or heavily reworked. Even if genuine, the term is used in a cosmological sense (the cosmos as active process), not yet in the technical Aristotelian sense of actuality vs. potentiality.

Anaxagoras (fl. -470) — Testimonies (btk-aa, sid 1671919)

AËT. V 25, 2: Ἀναξαγόρας κατὰ κόπον τῆς σωματικῆς ἐνεργείας γίνεσθαι τὸν ὕπνον "Anaxagoras: sleep comes about from weariness of bodily activity"

#energeia #testimonia Again Aëtius, using the term in a physiological sense (bodily exertion). Not Anaxagoras' own wording.

Empedocles (fl. -444) — On Nature and Perception (die-ab, sid 1686632)

Simpl. de caelo 528, 30: ἐν ἄλλοις τὴν ἀμφοῖν ἐν τοῖς αὐτοῖς ἐνέργειαν παραδίδωσι "In other places he presents the activity of both in the same things"

#energeia #testimonia Simplicius (6th c. CE) uses the term in his commentary to describe Empedocles' account of Love and Strife. The word is Simplicius' own analytical vocabulary, not Empedocles' verse.

Leucippus (fl. -430) — Testimonies (dra-aa, sid 1687521)

ARISTOT. Metaph. Λ 6. 1071b 31: διὸ ἔνιοι ποιοῦσιν ἀεὶ ἐνέργειαν οἷον Λεύκιππος καὶ Πλάτων· αἰεὶ γὰρ εἶναί φασι κίνησιν "Hence some make activity/actuality always exist, e.g. Leucippus and Plato; for they say motion always exists"

#energeia #finding This is Aristotle himself attributing the concept (though not necessarily the word) to Leucippus and Plato. Aristotle says they "make ἐνέργεια always exist" — meaning they posited eternal motion. But the passage is Aristotle's own characterisation, not a quotation. And Plato does not use the word ἐνέργεια anywhere in the corpus.

Metrodorus of Chios (fl. -430) — Testimonies (dmk-aa, sid 1119783)

Μητρόδωρος μηδὲν ἐν τῶι οἰκείωι τόπωι σῶμα κινεῖσθαι, εἰ μή τις προώσειεν ἢ καθελκύσειε κατ' ἐνέργειαν "Metrodorus: no body moves in its proper place unless someone pushes or pulls it in actuality"

#energia #testimonia Again a doxographic report (Aëtius), using the Aristotelian distinction δυνάμει / ἐνεργείᾳ.

Trophonius (fl. -420) — Introduction to Rhetoric (cda-aa, sid 782022)

τοῦ γὰρ ὄντος διπλοῦν τὸ σημαινόμενον ἔχοντος καὶ πῂ μὲν τὸ ἐνεργείᾳ, πῂ δὲ τὸ δυνάμει σημαίνοντος "For 'what is' has a double meaning, signifying in one way what is in actuality, in another what is potentially"

#energeia #finding This is a rhetorical text that uses the Aristotelian δυνάμει/ἐνεργείᾳ distinction. The author Trophonius is dated fl. -420 to -370 in the corpus metadata, but the content is clearly post-Aristotelian (the δυνάμει/ἐνεργείᾳ distinction is Aristotle's signature contribution). This text is likely misdated or pseudepigraphic.

Alcidamas (fl. -432) — Odysseus (dkm-aa, sid 840565)

γεγραμμένος εἰκόνι λόγου τὴν φύσιν ὁμοίαν ἔχων ἁπάσης ἐνεργείας ἄμοιρος καθέστηκεν "A written speech, having a nature similar to a painted image, stands deprived of all activity"

#energeia #finding Alcidamas uses ἐνέργεια in a rhetorical sense: a written speech lacks the "liveliness" or "active force" of an extemporaneous oral speech. This is the rhetorical sense of ἐνέργεια (vividness, actuality in performance), not yet the metaphysical one.

3. Plato — zero occurrences
#energeia #finding Plato does not use the noun ἐνέργεια in any of its forms anywhere in the indexed corpus. The verb ἐνεργέω is also absent. This is a significant negative result: the term that Aristotle would make central to his metaphysics is entirely absent from Plato's vocabulary.

Conclusion
#energeia #decision The pre-Aristotelian use of ἐνέργεια is real but thin. The only original (non-doxographic) pre-Aristotelian uses are:

Aesop: everyday sense = "operative power" of birdlime (not philosophical)
Alcidamas: rhetorical sense = "liveliness" of oral speech (not metaphysical)
Philolaus (if genuine): cosmological sense = "eternal activity" of the cosmos
Every other pre-Aristotelian occurrence is a doxographer's report using Aristotelian vocabulary to describe an earlier thinker — the word belongs to the reporter, not the reported.

#energeia #open Aristotle is the first to make ἐνέργεια a technical philosophical term — the correlate of δύναμις, the defining mark of actuality over potentiality, the activity that is the being-at-work of a thing. The pre-Aristotelian evidence shows the word existed in ordinary Greek (meaning "activity, operation, working") and in rhetorical theory (meaning "vividness"), but no earlier thinker had elevated it to the central metaphysical role it plays in Aristotle.
