# Τεκμήριον · φιλοστοργία

*A corpus-grounded diachronic survey. Stoic usage taken as the pivot, with the pre-Stoic ground and the post-Stoic spread laid out on either side. Built by pure word-search of the φιλοστοργ- family across the whole Eulogikon corpus (400 attestations of the concept, 98 authors, archaic through late-antique), tagged after collection, clustered by Greek collocation. Every passage is cited by its Eulogikon work-id (eul_wid) and reference. Ecclesiastical and Byzantine material is set aside from the reading and kept only as data.*

---

## The word and its root

φιλοστοργία/philostorgia is a doubled affection-word. Its root is the verb στέργω/stergō (to cherish, to be content with, to hold fast to what is one's own), which the corpus attests 1095 times, with the noun στοργή/storgē (205 times) naming the attachment itself. On that root the corpus builds the adjective φιλόστοργος/philostorgos, and only then, by derivation, the abstract noun. The grammarians say so directly. The Etymologicum work (eul_wid xie-aa, φ 152) rules that the comparative is drawn off the adjective, not compounded afresh:

Φιλοστοργότερος· Ἀπὸ τοῦ φιλόστοργος. Οὐ γάρ ἐστι σύνθετον, ἀλλὰ παρασύνθετον.

Philostorgóteros: apò toû philóstorgos. Ou gár esti sýntheton, allà parasýntheton.

Philostorgóteros is from philóstorgos. For it is not a compound but a derived-compound.

The lexicographers gloss the adjective with a single narrower word. Hesychius (eul_wid taq-ac, phi 518) writes:

φιλόστοργος· φιλότεκνος.

philóstorgos: philóteknos.

philóstorgos: child-cherishing.

So the corpus itself, at its lexicographic edge, reads the word as cherishing pointed first at offspring. The survey that follows tests whether that narrow gloss holds across the whole span, and where the word reaches past it.

The internal etymology is visible inside a philosophical fragment as well. Chrysippus (eul_wid kms-ae, ref 292), listing the affections that stand near ἀγάπη/agápē, defines φιλοστοργία through the root verb itself:

ἥ τε φιλοστοργία φιλοτεχνία τις οὖσα περὶ στέρξιν φίλων.

hḗ te philostorgía philotechnía tis oûsa perì stérxin phílōn.

and philostorgía, being a kind of devoted craft concerned with the cherishing (stérxis) of one's own.

The compound is thus tied back to στέργω/stergō by a Stoic hand: φιλοστοργία is the settled disposition of στέρξις toward those who are οἰκεῖοι, one's own.

## Earliest attestations

The earliest station is not a philosopher's definition but a letter of household counsel. Theano of Croton (eul_wid bhc-aa, ref 199), in the archaic Pythagorean material, sets φιλοστοργία as one of the wife's four dispositions, and fixes its object at once:

τῇ δὲ φιλοστοργίᾳ περὶ τὰ τέκνα.

tê(i) dè philostorgía(i) perì tà tékna.

and by cherishing, toward the children.

Already the collocate that the late lexicographers will isolate (περὶ τὰ τέκνα/perì tà tékna, toward the children) is present at the first attestation. The archaic count is small in raw terms (three tokens), but against the tiny surviving archaic volume it is the second-densest period in the survey.

The classical period keeps the object and adds the qualifier that will become the Stoic hinge: κατὰ φύσιν/katà phýsin, the attachment a creature grows into rather than one it chooses. Xenophon (eul_wid ezq-ag, ref Cyr.1.3.2) writes it of the child Cyrus meeting his grandfather:

οἷα δὴ παῖς φύσει φιλόστοργος ὢν ἠσπάζετό τε αὐτόν.

hoîa dḕ paîs phýsei philóstorgos ṑn ēspázetó te autón.

being, as a child is, cherishing by his own phýsis, he embraced him.

Aristotle carries the same qualifier out of the household and into the herd. In the zoological survey (eul_wid hgw-av, ref HA 611a) the word describes animals, and the evidence he gives is that mares will rear one another's foals:

δοκεῖ τὸ τῶν ἵππων γένος εἶναι φύσει φιλόστοργον.

dokeî tò tôn híppōn génos eînai phýsei philóstorgon.

the horse-kind seems to be cherishing by its own phýsis.

This is the load-bearing pre-Stoic finding. Before any school makes it doctrine, φιλοστοργία is already (a) directed downward at offspring, (b) κατὰ φύσιν, and (c) observed in animals as much as in people. The Stoa does not invent the concept. It inherits it and puts weight on it.

## The Stoic pivot

For the Stoa φιλοστοργία is not one topic among many. It is the empirical hinge of οἰκείωσις/oikeíōsis, the doctrine that a creature is bound to what is its own. The affection parents feel for offspring is the visible proof that this binding reaches past self-preservation to the care of others. Chrysippus states it as fact of phýsis (eul_wid kms-ae, ref 731):

φασὶ δὲ καὶ τὴν πρὸς τὰ τέκνα φιλοστοργίαν φυσικὴν εἶναι αὐτοῖς.

phasì dè kaì tḕn pròs tà tékna philostorgían physikḕn eînai autoîs.

and they say that the cherishing toward one's children is, for them, a thing of phýsis.

Antipater of Tarsus (eul_wid lcc-aa, ref 62) then marks the danger built into it. The same attachment, taken to excess, swerves a person off what is useful:

ἀποκεκλικότες ἀπὸ τοῦ συμφέροντος διὰ τὴν ἄγαν φιλοστοργίαν.

apokeklikótes apò toû symphérontos dià tḕn ágan philostorgían.

swerving away from what is useful through excessive cherishing.

Posidonius (eul_wid msa-ab, ref 417) widens the object from the household to the ground that reared one, keeping the word but stretching its reach:

οὐκ ἐπιλανθάνονται τῆς πρὸς τὴν θρέψασαν γῆν φιλοστοργίας.

ouk epilanthánontai tês pròs tḕn thrépsasan gên philostorgías.

they do not forget the cherishing toward the land that nurtured them.

The set-piece is Epictetus, Discourse 1.11 (eul_wid ojw-ac, ref 1.11), an entire conversation built on the word. Epictetus does not deny the affection. He tests whether it can be κατὰ φύσιν and also εὐλόγιστον/eulógiston, well-reasoned, at the same time:

τὸ φιλόστοργον δοκεῖ σοι κατὰ φύσιν τ᾽ εἶναι καὶ καλόν;

tò philóstorgon dokeî soi katà phýsin t᾽ eînai kaì kalón?

does the cherishing seem to you to be katà phýsin and good?

The interlocutor grants it. Epictetus then closes the trap he has laid, that a father who flees his sick child out of affection has not acted κατὰ φύσιν at all, because the affection and the flight cannot both be well-reasoned:

μὴ τοίνυν μάχην ἔχει τῷ φιλοστόργῳ τὸ εὐλόγιστον;

mḕ toínyn máchēn échei tô(i) philostórgō(i) tò eulógiston?

does the well-reasoned, then, have any quarrel with the cherishing?

The answer is that it does not. The Stoic move is not to cut φιλοστοργία away but to require that it keep company with reason. Where it does not, Epictetus refuses it the name. In Discourse 3.24 (eul_wid ojw-ac, ref 3.24) he interrogates the word directly, when a man calls his grief for absent kin φιλοστοργία:

εἶτά μοι καλεῖς τοῦτο φιλοστοργίαν; ποίαν, ἄνθρωπε, φιλοστοργίαν;

eîtá moi kaleîs toûto philostorgían? poían, ánthrōpe, philostorgían?

and you call this cherishing? what kind, man, of cherishing?

That is the sharpened Stoic sense: the affection is kept, its excess (the ἄγαν/ágan of Antipater) is stripped off as the enslaving thing it becomes. Marcus Aurelius states the reconciled position in one phrase, praising a man who was, at once, most free of πάθος and most cherishing (eul_wid qpy-aa, ref 1.9.1):

ἅμα μὲν ἀπαθέστατον εἶναι, ἅμα δὲ φιλοστοργότατον.

háma mèn apathéstaton eînai, háma dè philostorgótaton.

to be at once most free of páthos and most cherishing.

And Marcus turns the adverb into a rule for how to correct others, cherishingly and without a sting (eul_wid qpy-aa, ref 11.18.4):

ἀλλὰ φιλοστόργως καὶ ἀδήκτως τῇ ψυχῇ.

allà philostórgōs kaì adḗktōs tê(i) psychê(i).

but cherishingly and without a bite to the psychḗ.

The privative shows the same commitment from its shadow side. Epictetus (eul_wid ojw-ac, ref 2.17) reports the reproach a dying parent's kin throw at the absent Stoic, ἀφιλόστοργος γέρων/aphilóstorgos gérōn, an uncherishing old man. The word for the absence is coined off the same root, and it is felt as a real charge, not a matter of indifference.

## The post-Stoic spread

Around and after the Stoa, the word runs wide, and its densest attestation is the Roman imperial period (about 2.0 tokens per million characters, the peak of the whole survey). Three registers stand out.

In historiography the object shifts from child to dynast. Josephus (eul_wid ofq-aa, ref BJ 1 523) uses it of Herod, cherishing toward one son alone:

πανταχοῦ δ᾽ ἀμείλικτον εὑρίσκειν τὸν πατέρα καὶ μόνῳ φιλόστοργον Ἀντιπάτρῳ.

pantachoû d᾽ ameílikton heurískein tòn patéra kaì mónō(i) philóstorgon Antipátrō(i).

everywhere he found his father unappeasable, and cherishing toward Antipater alone.

In medicine and the literature of exemplary devotion the word marks attachment carried to death. Galen (eul_wid qmm-dn, ref 14 236) reports the wife who chose to die with her husband:

μέχρι τοῦ συναποθανεῖν αὐτῇ τοσαύτην φιλοστοργίαν.

méchri toû synapothaneîn autê(i) tosaútēn philostorgían.

so great a cherishing as to die together with her.

And in the philosophical prose of the wider imperial schools the Stoic-adjacent sense holds. Plutarch, who carries the largest single share of the corpus attestations, keeps the word in its affection-network, pairing it with the erotic and companionable dispositions rather than the harsh ones (eul_wid okg-ae, ref 56 C). The word reaches the Neoplatonic commentators too: Porphyry (eul_wid udm-ac, ref 10 252-253) uses φιλοστοργία of a ruler's studied fondness for a crowd, a political stretching of the household word.

Across this spread the object of the cherishing loosens (child, then kin, then homeland, benefactor, ruler, companion), while the core holds: an attachment that is felt as given rather than chosen, and that is tested precisely when it costs.

The reading of the shape: the concept is stable from Theano onward in its core (cherishing of one's own, above all offspring, κατὰ φύσιν). What moves is not the core but its edges: the range of licit objects widens over time, and the philosophical treatment sharpens in the Stoa, where the affection is affirmed and its excess named. The imperial-period density peak is where the greatest weight of attention falls, driven by the moralists and historians, not by a change in what the word means.

## Crossing phrase

> ἡ κατὰ φύσιν στέρξις πρὸς τοὺς οἰκείους, μάλιστα πρὸς τὰ τέκνα, ἣ οὐκ ἐκ προαιρέσεως γίνεται ἀλλὰ συναύξεται, καὶ διαμένει διὰ τῶν χαλεπῶν.
>
> *hē katà phýsin stérxis pròs toùs oikeíous, málista pròs tà tékna, hḕ ouk ek prohairéseōs gínetai allà synaúxetai, kaì diaménei dià tôn chalepôn.*
>
> the cherishing of one's own, above all of one's offspring, that does not come by decision but grows up together with the creature, and stays fast through hardship (gloss, not the working artifact).

The phrase is built only from vocabulary the attestations themselves supply: στέρξις from Chrysippus, κατὰ φύσιν from Xenophon, Aristotle and Epictetus, τέκνα/οἰκεῖοι from Theano and the Stoa, the testing-through-hardship from Epictetus and Galen. It covers the concept across at least six domains and four periods.

## Outliers and caveats

The personal names Φιλόστοργος and Φιλοστόργιος (a man is called prosperous by Epictetus at eul_wid ojw-ac, ref 3.17; the historian Philostorgius elsewhere) fold to the same string and are held apart in the data by a lexical-status flag; they are not the concept and are not counted here. The New Testament ἀφιλόστοργος (Romans 1:31, 2 Timothy 3:3) is a genuine attestation of the privative but belongs to a strand the survey method sets aside from the reading; it is noted only as a parallel. The Byzantine lexica (Suda, Photius, the Etymologica) are used above only for the root-gloss and the grammatical note, not developed as a period of the concept's life.

Two limits on confidence. First, time-ordering here is author-level (by floruit), not passage-level, so the sequence within a single busy author is not resolved. Second, vector coverage of the corpus was partial at last check (about 14.6 percent, pre-clean-text), so the survey is flagged PROVISIONAL: the crossing holds across the spread shown, but it should be re-run against term_distribution and search_text before being promoted. Nothing here rests on a translation choice; where a word (φύσις, πάθος, ψυχή) would be flattened by an English rendering, it is left in Greek with a bracketed gloss.
